Inflation Uncertainty and Monetary Policy Announcements

Mardoqueo Arteaga
Work in Progress, 2020

Preview

Household expectations about economic conditions, such as inflation, drive a wide range of decisions that include saving, borrowing, and consumption. The transmission of monetary policy, then, rests on understanding how these expectations change, and therefore the transparency of central bank communication is key in aligning both sides. Inflation expectations, specifically, represent a key variable that the central bank monitors carefully. In theory, transparent communication makes policy decisions more predictable and creates more policy options at the effective lower bound ( Blinder, 2009), but in practice, this effectiveness hinges on the communication actually changing beliefs of economic agents in the right direction and in the adequate amount of time. There is a growing understanding of how communication, or announcements in the form of news, by the central bank affects expectations of financial market participants and professional forecasters ( (Campbell, Evans, Fisher, & Justiniano, 2012), (Negro, Giannoni, & Patterson, 2012), (Nakamura & Steinsson, 2018)), but much less is known about the role of announcements on the beliefs of the general public.

In this paper, I ask whether monetary policy announcements have a meaningful impact on household inflation beliefs, specifically in their uncertainty about future macroeconomic conditions, using a well-established monthly survey of public beliefs, the Survey of Consumer Expectations (SCE) by the Federal Reserve Bank of New York. The SCE began in June 2013 and is a nationally representative online survey comprising of a 12-month rotating group of approximately 1,300 household heads. The surveys objective is to solicit households for a variety of quantitative measures for a wide range of economic outcomes (e.g. inflation, income, household finance, and employment). Armantier, Topa, et al (2017) provide the specific components of expectations solicitation.

In order to account for potential joint effects with the exogenous monetary policy shocks and to address the foregoing patterns, I regress the inflation expectations on the lagged shocks and individual characteristics. \[ E^h Infl_{t} = \beta_{0} + \beta_{1} MP_{t-1} + \beta_{2} \mathbf{X}_{h,t} + \lambda_{h} + \epsilon_{h,t} \]

where the dependent variable, \(E^h Infl_{t}\), takes on the form of the \(h\) household's median point forecast for inflation expectations at the 12- and 36-month horizon as well as the month over month adjustment for both forecasts (a total of four distinct dependent variables whose value is derived at the month of the survey \(t\)).

The lagged monetary policy shock \(MP_{t-1}\) consists of the Federal Funds Shock and the Policy News Shock. A vector of controls is also included that contains the aforementioned variables and are collected from the household \(h\) at the month of the survey \(t\). \(\lambda_h\) accounts for household fixed effects. Then, the \(\beta_1\) is the coefficient of interest and will represent the average impact the monetary policy shock has on the dependent variables of household inflation expectations. An additional exploration will include heterogeneous impacts of monetary policy by age and take on the form \[ E^h Infl_{t} = \beta_{0} + \beta_{1} MP_{t-1}\times Age_{h} + Age_{h} + \beta_{2} \mathbf{X}_{h,t} + \lambda_{h} + \epsilon_{h,t} \]
